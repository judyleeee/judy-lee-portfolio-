## Context · Interactive Gift-Giving Quiz UX

**Background.** Winners is one of Canada's largest off-price fashion retailers · and it has no e-commerce site. Every digital touchpoint carries disproportionate weight because there is no other way to test digital behavior, capture intent, or build CRM from this brand.

For the Christmas Big Black Bag campaign, a kiosk-based interactive gift quiz was designed for a 2-day pop-up in Toronto. The kiosk wasn't a campaign asset · it was **the only digital feedback loop the brand had**.

**Business goals:** brand engagement, in-store traffic, and email capture · not direct purchase conversion.

**The experience.** 5 questions guide users to personalized gift ideas. The result screen offers three paths: email results, find a nearby store, or try again. Distributed via on-site kiosk and QR-to-mobile, so the same flow had to hold up across two contexts.

My scope covered **branding**, the **interactive quiz UX/UI**, and the **supporting site**. The physical booth build was handled by a separate team.

## Solution 1 · Cover & Progress UX

**The core hesitation: not knowing what this is.** One-line copy communicates purpose instantly. A single CTA lowers the cost of starting.

**Three design decisions targeted the entry drop-off:**

- **EN/FR toggle** for Canadian bilingual context
- **UX writing** that places purpose and value in one line
- **Popup, not new tab**, to prevent window-close issues on the kiosk hardware

## Solution 2 & 3 · CTA hierarchy

**"Send this to me"** leads as the primary CTA · it's what the user came for. Email input unlocks the subscription prompt naturally · business goal served as a consequence of value, not a gate. **"Find another gift"** drives re-engagement without dead-ending the flow.

Mid-quiz, the progress bar and back-navigation returned control to the user · resolving the anxiety named in QA. The flow itself dropped from **6 to 5 steps** · every removed step was a removed drop-off opportunity.

**Quantitative**

- **46% completion rate** · voluntary · no incentive · no purchase path. Achieved by reducing the flow from 6 to 5 steps and lowering the cost of starting.
- **300+ sessions · 1,225 page views** over 2 days, in a walk-by pop-up with no staff direction · the screen itself had to earn every tap.
- **25% email opt-in** · 1-in-4 completers emailed their results. For a retailer with no e-commerce, this is a rare digital signal tied directly to in-store purchase intent.

**Qualitative · QA-validated**

- **Resilience in retail.** Despite cold weather and on-site wait times later in the day, the majority of users finished the quiz.
- **Anxiety neutralized.** QA feedback shifted from "Can I go back?" to "Love that I can go back" · the progress bar and back-navigation directly resolved the named friction.

## What I'd Test Next · From a 2-day pop-up to a learning system

<Callout>
  The 25% email opt-in is not just a CRM number · it's a feedback loop a model could learn from within a single campaign cycle.
</Callout>

- **Integrate AI-powered personalization into the quiz logic.** Product recommendations dynamically ranked by real-time inventory and seasonal performance data, not static rules. Each session feeds the next.
- **Extend the physical-to-digital journey.** Next iteration would follow users from in-store kiosk → email → store visit, measuring the full loop.
- **Design analytics structure upfront.** Drop-off tracking per step, session replay on consent, A/B on the cover screen copy.

## Reflection

**What I'd do differently.** I'd define the design system before the first screen · kiosk, mobile, and web each needed different component structures, and resolving that mid-build cost time that could have been spent on the edges of the experience.

**What I learned.**

- **Physical constraints were the brief.** Kiosk hardware, short dwell time, and bilingual requirements were both the limitation and the design brief · the system was shaped by what the environment made impossible, not by what was visually preferred.
- **Anxiety, not difficulty, drives drop-off.** Users didn't struggle with the quiz · they feared commitment to an unknown flow. The fix was control, not simplification.
- **When business goals compete, users choose none.** Three equally-weighted CTAs produced paralysis · sequencing them by user need first, business goal second, turned the result screen into a funnel instead of a menu.
